It’s been about three months since I last wrote an update.

Over the past few months, I’ve been jumping across a lot of ideas, prototypes, and directions. Some of them felt promising immediately, some turned out more complicated than I expected, and some were useful mostly because they helped me understand what not to force right now. This update is mostly a reflection on that process: what I’ve been building, what feels clearer now, and how I’m thinking about the road ahead.

One thing I spent time exploring was the [Crux & Beta iOS app](https://cruxbeta.dev/?article=2026-01-11), especially around bringing AR into the daily climbing gym experience. The prototype itself was exciting. Being able to point a phone at the wall and have problem information appear in place feels genuinely interesting, and I still think there’s something there. But after pushing on it more, I ran into two issues. One is product scope: if this stays primarily iOS-native and AR-driven, it becomes a pretty narrow entry point. The other, more serious one, is technical. Once too many wall references are stored and matched on-device, performance starts to fall apart, and that immediately hurts the experience. I also don’t have enough ARKit depth right now to justify spending a huge amount of time trying to optimize that system further. So for now, I’m treating it as a proof of concept that worked — and one that I’m happy I explored — but not something I want to center the product around today.

At the same time, something else became much clearer.

---

While working on an application for Founders Inc’s Canopy program, I had to answer a bunch of questions about what exactly I’m building, what the product is, and what makes the direction compelling. That process forced me to step back and describe the bigger picture more honestly. The line that feels most accurate right now is this: **Crux Beta Labs is building the operating system for modern rock climbing.**

That framing is intentionally ambitious, but I think it captures the shape of what I’ve actually been reaching toward. Not just a climbing journal. Not just coaching tools. Not just computer vision experiments. A broader system that starts with rock climbers first, then expands to route setters and climbing gyms, and maybe eventually even professional athletes–if it turns out the tools can help them improve in ways that are actually meaningful. I’m still a little hesitant to lean too hard on that last part right now, but I do think it’s worth leaving room for. Spray fits much more clearly into that picture now. It is the entry point, but not the full story. It is the first layer of a larger system.

That shift in framing also helped me think more clearly about what Spray should actually do. The core problem is not "make a climbing app." The core problem is to build a better interface for the real climbing workflow: finding climbs, remembering climbs, logging attempts, analyzing beta, tracking progress, and sharing the session in a way that feels natural. Around that core, there’s room for much more: image-based route discovery, movement analysis, social sharing, visuals, gym-facing tools, and other climbing-specific products that feel meaningfully better than what exists now.

---

Another direction I’ve been thinking about is something I’m calling **Navigator** for now. This is more focused on outdoor climbing. The rough idea is a climbing-specific navigation tool for finding outdoor boulders and climbing spots. Instead of relying only on a traditional map, I’m imagining a hybrid interface: compass-style guidance while you’re still some distance away, and then more spatial guidance–potentially AR–once you get closer to the actual destination. A lot of that thinking comes from real experience. I remember being at Holy Boulders in southern Illinois and how surprisingly difficult it was to find the actual boulders once you were out there, especially with weak signal and confusing approaches. I’m not interested in replacing the human part of climbing — asking other climbers, sharing knowledge, learning from the community. But I do think there’s room for tools that lower the friction and make those experiences easier to navigate.

---

So this past stretch has been a lot of rethinking. A lot of commits, a lot of experiments, and a lot of time spent trying to understand what deserves to be pushed forward and what should stay as a future layer. The AR work was worth doing, even if partly because it showed me both the possibilities and the limits. Spray feels clearer now. The larger company vision feels clearer too. And I think the main thing I’m taking from the last few months is that stepping back to name the bigger shape of the system was necessary.
